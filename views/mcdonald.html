How we started

The process of ordering food at a fast-food restaurant is supposed to be quick and easy, but with high demand creating long lines, the process has become more difficult and frustrating. In order to resolve this issue and create an efficient and easy way to order, McDonald’s created a kiosk. These kiosks allow customers to order their food manually by touching a screen. In theory, the idea of a kiosk is a great one that should work, but unfortunately, there are many issues that come with it. All of our group members had bad experience at the McDonald's at Nobel Dr. near UCSD, especially with the kiosks.

Key Problems

  • People often make mistakes while using the kiosks.
  • The mistakes and confusions slow down ordering process.
  • Kiosk are not actually more convinient than ordering with cashier.

Methodology

Our first attempts at data collection were done using a Master-Apprentice Method of focused observation. We sought to carefully observe user experience with the kiosk in two ways.

Our first method of gathering data was to speak to the consumer directly and give them tasks in order to get a full range of activity on the kiosk. We will also record our volunteers' operation with their and McDonald's permission. Due to the project nature, we only conduct 15 surveys and interviews.

Since we can't guarantee all interviewees will let us record their operation, we make a alternative plan, which is everyone observe and mark down the errors they made.

Among the errors we collected from 15 participants, the most common errors are mistapping, lagging, and can't find items. The occurance of these errors are quite high that nearly everyone made once.

Mistapping

Situation 1: users tapped the wrong place by accident, then they tapped the right place to finish the intended action. For example, as shown in Gif, user WZ was asked to order a “10pcs McNuggets Meal''. After he clicked on the item, a window popped up asking “would you like to add-on”. Instead of tapping the “make it a meal” button, he tapped the “just the item” button. He then realized that he clicked the wrong button and returned to the previous step immediately.

Situation 2: the kiosks’ screens aren’t very sensitive to the touches so sometimes they won’t react to gentle touches, so users have to touch the screen multiple times to elicit kiosks’ responses. As shown in Gif, user WZ’s first tap wasn’t sensed by the kiosk, so he had to tap harder on the screen to make it respond.

Can't find item

Users navigated through the menu for a long time to find the items.

12 of the 15 interviewees experience these problems during the tasks. In the designer’s mental model, users can go straight to the correct category to find what they want. However in reality, users reported that they didn’t know which categories the items were in, so they had to look through multiple categories to find the items. For example, we found that all 15 interviewees have a hard time looking for hash browns and only 5 were able to locate them. This trend occurs because hash browns are not in any categories listed on the left side of the screen, and users have to go to the home screen and then go to the “All Day Breakfast” category to find it.

Cup confusion & Tent Card confusion

Users navigated through the menu for a long time to find the items.

5 of the 15 interviewees didn’t know where to find cups and 7 of the 15 interviewees didn’t know where to find the tent cards. Before checking out, users were asked to “Grab a Table tend and enter the code” (Figure 2.3). However, the kiosks didn't tell users where the tent cards were and what code to enter, and there’s no option to skip this step and continue to checkout (nothing happened if the users tapped the “Continue” button without entering a code). The kiosks also told nothing about how and where to pick up drinks, creating confusion for the users.
